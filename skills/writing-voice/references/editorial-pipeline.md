# Editorial Pipeline: The McPhee Loop

Five editorial passes applied sequentially. Each pass has a distinct lens and concrete checklist. The order matters: logic before structure, structure before sentences, sentences before rhetoric.

## When to Use

- **Blog posts**: All 5 passes
- **LinkedIn 400+ words**: Passes 1-4
- **LinkedIn <400 words**: Passes 1 + 3 + 4 (closer only)
- **Threads**: Passes 1-4 + thread arc check
- **Tweets**: Do NOT use this pipeline. Use Compression method from generative-methods.md.

---

## Pass 1: Minto (Logical Architecture)

**Lens:** Does the argument hold together? Is every section earning its place logically?

**Checklist:**

1. State the governing thought (thesis) in one sentence. If you cannot, the argument is not clear enough yet. Stop and clarify before continuing.

2. Apply SCQ:
   - **Situation**: What does the reader already know or accept?
   - **Complication**: What has changed, gone wrong, or created tension?
   - **Question**: What question does the reader now have that this piece answers?
   - Write each in one sentence. If any takes more than one sentence, it is not sharp enough.

3. For each section after the opening, answer: "What question would the reader naturally have at this point?" If the section does not answer that question, it is in the wrong place or should not exist.

4. Check argument grouping: Are supporting points logically grouped? Do they progress (general to specific, or chronological, or by importance)? If the order could be random, the logic is weak.

5. Find vague assertions masquerading as insight. "Data quality matters" is vague. "Data quality degrades 3% per month without active governance" is an argument. Sharpen or cut.

**Output:** Draft where every section has a logical reason to exist and follows from the previous one.

---

## Pass 2: McPhee (Structure & Discovery)

**Lens:** Does the structure serve the argument? Does the piece have momentum?

**Checklist:**

1. **The Lead.** Read the opening 1-3 sentences. Does the lead promise the reader something worth reading for? A lead should create tension, curiosity, or a question — not explain what the post is about. If the lead is a summary, rewrite it as a promise.

2. **Selection.** For each section, ask: what is included and what is excluded? Is the selection serving the argument, or is it everything you know about the topic? Cut the "everything I know" sections. Keep the "this is what matters" sections.

3. **Juxtaposition.** Are contrasting ideas placed next to each other for effect? Are similar ideas separated enough to avoid redundancy? Move sections if juxtaposition would sharpen the argument.

4. **Greening.** Find every transition, seam, and join between sections. For each: can it be cut so the reader moves naturally from one section to the next without a signpost? Remove "In addition," "Furthermore," "Another point" — if the structure is right, the reader does not need directions.

5. **Forward momentum.** Read the piece end to end. Does each section create a question that the next section answers? If a section creates no forward pull, it is a dead spot. Cut or reposition.

6. **The delete test.** For each paragraph: would the piece survive without it? If yes, consider cutting. The piece should be as long as it needs to be and no longer.

**Output:** Restructured draft with strong lead, clean selections, invisible seams, forward momentum.

---

## Pass 3: Zinsser (Sentence Craft)

**Lens:** Is every word earning its place? Is the prose clear, active, and rhythmic?

**Checklist:**

1. **Clutter hit list.** Find and eliminate:
   - Adverbs modifying strong verbs ("ran quickly" → "sprinted")
   - Qualifiers: somewhat, rather, quite, very, really, a bit, slightly
   - Throat-clearing: actually, basically, essentially, fundamentally, in order to, the fact that, it should be noted that, it is worth mentioning
   - Redundant pairs: each and every, first and foremost, various different
   - Filler phrases: "I think that", "it is important to note that", "at the end of the day"

2. **Passive to active.** Find every passive construction. Convert to active unless the passive is deliberately hiding the agent for effect. "The decision was made" → "We decided" or "The CEO decided."

3. **Verb strength.** Replace weak verbs + adverb with strong verbs. Replace "is/was/are" constructions with action verbs where possible. "The team was responsible for" → "The team owned."

4. **Sentence length variation.** Read the paragraph aloud mentally. Three long sentences in a row create drag. Three short sentences in a row create staccato. Alternate. Follow a complex sentence with a simple one. Use a short sentence for punch after a long buildup.

5. **Word count check.** Compare to pre-Zinsser draft. Target: 70-80% of previous word count. If you have not cut 20-30%, you were not aggressive enough.

**Output:** Draft that is 20-30% shorter, sharper, more active, with varied rhythm.

---

## Pass 4: Forsyth (Rhetorical Technique)

**Lens:** Are there moments where a rhetorical device would elevate the writing? Applied surgically, not sprayed.

**Hard constraint:** At most 1 rhetorical device per 200 words. If the device calls attention to itself rather than the idea, remove it immediately.

**Checklist:**

1. **Tricolon (rule of three).** Scan for lists or series. Groups of three have natural rhythm and memorability. "Learn to sell. Learn to build. If you can do both, you will be unstoppable." Use for key points or closers. Do not force — only where three genuine items exist.

2. **Antithesis.** Scan for contrast points. Antithesis places opposites in parallel structure for clarity and force. "Not X, but Y." "Data informs. Humans decide." This is natural to Aswin's voice — the contrast pair is already a signature move.

3. **Periodic sentence.** For one key moment (usually the climax or the closing), consider delaying the main clause to build tension. "After six months of building, three pivots, and one near-death experience — we shipped." Use at most once per piece.

4. **Anadiplosis.** Repeating the end of one clause at the start of the next for emphasis. "The model predicted churn. Churn that had already happened." Use sparingly — once per piece at most.

5. **The closing line.** Read the last sentence. Is it quotable? Does it land? If not, apply one technique: a tricolon, an antithesis, or a reframe. The closing is the sentence most likely to be remembered. Earn it.

**Output:** Draft with 1-3 rhetorical moments that elevate without feeling forced or purple.

---

## Pass 5: McPhee Redux (800+ Words Only)

**Lens:** After sentence-level edits, does the structure still hold? Final coherence check.

Skip this pass for anything under 800 words. For short pieces, structural integrity does not degrade from tightening sentences.

**Checklist:**

1. Does the lead still work after all the sentence-level edits? Re-read it fresh. Does it still promise something the piece delivers?

2. Does the ending echo or answer the opening? The best pieces create a circle — the reader returns to the beginning with new understanding.

3. Final greening pass. Did the Zinsser cuts or Forsyth additions expose any seams between sections? Smooth them.

4. Read the full piece once more at speed. Where do you slow down or lose interest? Those are the remaining dead spots. Cut or tighten.

**Output:** Final draft with structural coherence verified after all sentence-level work.

---

## Common Pitfalls

- **Over-editing:** If you have run 5 passes and the piece feels lifeless, you have over-edited. Roll back to the post-Minto draft and take a lighter touch on passes 3-4.
- **Forsyth damage:** Rhetorical devices applied mechanically produce purple prose. If a device makes you wince, remove it. Trust plainness over forced eloquence.
- **Losing the voice:** The pipeline is editorial, not generative. If the output sounds like "good writing" but not like Aswin, the voice check in SKILL.md has failed. Go back to voice-and-beliefs.md and recalibrate.
- **Zinsser overcutting:** Some apparent "clutter" is voice. Aswin's conversational register includes occasional qualifiers that make prose feel human. Do not strip all humanity in pursuit of efficiency.
